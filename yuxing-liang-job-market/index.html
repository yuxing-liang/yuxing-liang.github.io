Job Market Paper

Job Market Paper

Decision-Relevance and Irrelevant Questions: Deviation in Strategy-Proof Matching Mechanisms

Draft available upon request

Abstract

Strategy-proof matching mechanisms are designed so that truthful reporting is a dominant strategy. Yet in many real assignment environments, agents submit rich rank-order lists even though only a subset of the comparisons they report can affect their own assignment. This paper introduces a decision-relevance perspective for studying preference reports in matching mechanisms. A comparison is decision-relevant if changing it can alter the agent's allocation for some profile of other agents' reports; otherwise, the mechanism asks the agent to provide information that is payoff-irrelevant from the agent's own perspective.

The paper develops this idea theoretically for canonical assignment mechanisms and uses it to interpret observed deviations under strategy-proof mechanisms. The main argument is not that agents can profitably manipulate strategy-proof mechanisms, but that large report spaces contain many payoff-equivalent or nearly irrelevant dimensions. These dimensions can generate deviations that are behaviorally meaningful but not necessarily outcome-improving. The framework connects report-space complexity, priority structure, and preference-revelation behavior, and provides testable implications for laboratory and field assignment data.

Research

Research Agenda and Working Papers

My research agenda asks how institutions should elicit, interpret, and use preference information when agents face complicated assignment environments. I study both the formal properties of mechanisms and the empirical behavior of participants who interact with them.

Working Paper

Towards a Better Major Assignment: An Empirical Study with Students in China

with Xintong Han and Yan Zeng

This project studies major assignment in Chinese higher education using student preference data and mechanism-based counterfactual analysis. It evaluates how alternative assignment rules affect welfare, first-choice placement, distributional outcomes, and the incentives students face when submitting rank-order lists.

Work in Progress

Progressive Choices in School Choice Problems

with Szilvia Pápai

This paper studies school choice mechanisms that permit asymmetric choice opportunities under strict homogeneous priorities. The analysis connects progressive choice mechanisms to Immediate Acceptance and Serial Dictatorship and examines efficiency, manipulability, and justified envy.

Work in Progress

Bridging Theory and Evidence: How External Information Transforms Students' Application Decisions

This project investigates how information and application environments affect students' submitted preferences, assignment outcomes, and the empirical interpretation of preference-revelation behavior.
